Research

We apply mostly computational approaches, particularly those involving the analysis of high-throughput genomic and transcriptomic data, to fundamental questions in biomedical research, namely in the context of cancer, neurobiology, and ageing. By integrating transcriptomic data with molecular and clinical information, we aim to identify novel regulatory mechanisms and molecular targets across three interconnected research themes.

RNA in cancer
CDKN2A in senescence and cancer
alternative promoters senescence cancer transcriptomics
Members: Rita Martins-Silva, Maria Guerra (alumna), Mariana Ascensão-Ferreira (alumna), Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Collaborators: Jesús Gil, Maria Vivo
Read abstract

CDKN2A is a remarkable dual-coding locus encoding two structurally unrelated tumour suppressors (p16INK4A and p14ARF) through overlapping reading frames driven by distinct promoters. p16 inhibits CDK4/6 and is a well-established marker of cellular senescence; ARF stabilises p53 by restraining MDM2. Despite their centrality to cell cycle control, tumour suppression, and oncogene-induced senescence, remarkably little is known about how their expression is individually regulated across tissues, developmental stages, and disease contexts. Standard gene-centric transcriptomic analyses fail to discriminate between them, leaving a wealth of public RNA-seq, epigenomic, and genetic variation data largely unexploited for this purpose. The locus' evolutionary history is similarly understudied, with longstanding hypotheses about asymmetric divergence between the two reading frames yet to be tested at the tissue- and condition-specific expression level. This project addresses these gaps by integrating comparative transcriptomics, epigenomic profiling, and eQTL analyses to characterise how p16 and ARF are independently regulated and how their relative expression shapes senescence programmes and cancer vulnerability across tissues and species.

RNA in cancer
Uncovering the stressome: stress granule signature and its implication in cancer
stress granules cancer transcriptomics epigenetics
Members: Alexandre Kaizeler, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Collaborators: Vanessa Morais
Read abstract

Stress granules (SGs) are membraneless cytoplasmic condensates that form rapidly in response to a wide range of cellular stressors, including hypoxia, nutrient deprivation, oxidative stress, viral infection, and chemotherapy, and dissolve upon stress resolution. Composed of stalled preinitiation complexes, mRNAs, and a rich complement of RNA-binding proteins, they are thought to selectively sequester specific transcripts and proteins, including regulators of apoptosis, senescence, and immune signalling, thereby fine-tuning the cellular response to environmental challenge. Beyond their physiological roles, SGs have been implicated in cancer, where they may promote survival and confer resistance to therapy, as well as in neurodegeneration and antiviral defence. Nevertheless, the precise composition of SGs and the functional significance of their molecular cargo remain contested, partly due to methodological inconsistencies between purification approaches. This project performs an unbiased integration of publicly available human SG transcriptomic datasets, spanning multiple cell lines, stressors, and purification methods, to resolve these discrepancies, identify reproducible features of SG-enriched transcriptomes, and probe the implications of SG biology for cancer and other pathological contexts.

Limited bone marrow chimerism impairs cell competition in the thymus and causes leukemia
cancer leukaemia cell competition immunodeficiency
Members: Francisca Xara-Brasil, Alexandre Kaizeler, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (co-PI)
Collaborators: Carolina Pinto, Vera Martins (PI)
Read abstract

Cell competition in the thymus is a critical tumour suppressor process that prevents leukemia. We show that suboptimal bone marrow correction of γc-deficient mice disrupts this process, inducing thymus autonomy and promoting T cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL). γc-deficiency causes severe combined immunodeficiency in mice and humans and is treated by hematopoietic stem and progenitor cell (HSPC) transplantation. However, inefficient correction led to intermittent thymic activity and the emergence of an aberrant thymocyte population that preceded overt leukemia. Leukemia incidence and latency were exacerbated when HSPCs were pre-cultured under conditions mimicking gene-editing protocols, whereas delaying thymopoietic support in the host mitigated disease. Critically, the extent of bone marrow reconstitution determined the ability to suppress thymus autonomy and leukemia development. These findings demonstrate that inadequate bone marrow correction directly compromises thymic cell competition, creating a permissive niche for malignant transformation, and highlight thymus autonomy as a risk in immunodeficiency treatment.

Neuro-biology Biostatistics
An effect size-based screening approach to identify actigraphy markers of the submarine environment
circadian rhythms actigraphy shift work sleep
Members: Daniel Marques, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Collaborators: Catia Reis (Co-PI)
Read abstract

Identifying behavioural markers of disease risk associated with circadian disruption is challenging due to reproducibility concerns in under-powered human studies. Submarines provide an extreme model of circadian disruption, depriving crew of natural light–dark cycles and limiting physical activity. We developed an effect size-based method to screen potential actigraphy-derived markers of the submarine environment, collecting data across pre-mission, mission, and post-mission periods. Forty-one actigraphy variables were screened for their ability to differentiate between study periods and shift types using Monte Carlo permutation testing and ROC curve analysis. Light-related features and DLMO trajectory slopes showed the strongest discriminatory potential. These findings support the use of actigraphy in dimly-lit, isolated, confined, and extreme (DICE) environments and its applicability to other operational or clinical populations with reduced activity.

Neuro-biology
Effect of statin treatment in chronic sleep deprivation
neurobiology sleep deprivation statins behaviour transcriptomics
Members: Alexandre Kaizeler, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (co-PI)
Collaborators: Gabriela Lima da Silva, Ana Sebastião (PI)
Read abstract

Statins are widely prescribed lipid-lowering drugs with well-established pleiotropic effects in the nervous system, including anti-inflammatory activity and modulation of neuronal signalling. Chronic sleep deprivation impairs cognitive function and alters brain gene expression, yet whether statin treatment can mitigate these consequences remains largely unexplored. Using a rat model of chronic sleep deprivation, this project investigates the effects of rosuvastatin, atorvastatin, and simvastatin on a battery of behavioural endpoints, including anxiety, locomotion, and spatial and recognition memory, alongside hippocampal transcriptomic profiling. Integrating differential gene expression and gene set enrichment analyses, we aim to characterise the molecular landscape of the sleep-deprived brain and its response to pharmacological intervention.

Bioinformatics
circStudio: a Python package for preprocessing, analyzing and modeling actigraphy data
actigraphy circadian rhythms Python wearables
Members: Daniel Marques, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Collaborators: Cátia Reis (Co-PI), Carina Fernandes
Read abstract

Actigraphy is a non-invasive and cost-effective method for monitoring behavioural rhythms under real-world conditions, collecting time-resolved measurements of locomotor activity, light exposure, and temperature. Although several open-source packages support specific aspects of actigraphy analysis, preprocessing, metric calculation, and mathematical modelling are often distributed across separate tools, limiting interoperability. circStudio is a Python package that unifies actigraphy data processing and mathematical modelling of circadian rhythms within a single framework. Built from the pyActigraphy codebase and integrating circadian models from the Arcascope circadian package, it provides flexible preprocessing tools, support for multiple actigraphy file formats, standalone functions for commonly used actigraphy metrics, and implementations of several mathematical models of circadian rhythms, enabling users to move efficiently from raw wearable data to physiologically interpretable circadian outputs.

Bioinformatics
scStudio: interactive interface for single-cell transcriptomics data exploration
scRNA-seq immuno-senescence
Members: Marta Bica, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Collaborators: Karine Serre
Read abstract

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has transformed our understanding of cellular heterogeneity, yet its analysis remains challenging for researchers without computational expertise. scStudio is a user-friendly, modular web-based application designed to democratise scRNA-seq data analysis. It supports automated dataset retrieval from the Gene Expression Omnibus, user-uploaded datasets in a variety of formats, multi-dataset integration, and flexible parameter optimisation. The application covers all essential steps: quality control, normalisation, dimensionality reduction, clustering, differential expression, and functional enrichment analysis, and includes analysis history tracking, session export, and data sharing features. scStudio is being applied to characterise immunosenescence at single-cell resolution.

Bioinformatics
CHARM: Comprehensive Hub for Alternative Regulatory Mapping
RNA-binding proteins alternative splicing ENCODE eCLIP regulatory networks
Members: Alexandre Kaizeler, Nuno Barbosa-Morais (PI)
Read abstract

RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) are key regulators of gene expression and RNA processing, yet the interplay between their roles in transcription, splicing, and direct RNA binding is not fully described. CHARM is an R Shiny web application that integrates multi-layered data from 168 RBPs profiled in the ENCODE project across HepG2 and K562 cell lines. CHARM enables exploration of RBP-driven changes in gene expression, alternative splicing, and RNA binding, and supports inference of regulatory networks across these layers. Splicing maps are generated through eCLIPSE, a companion tool that aligns eCLIP binding profiles to splicing event coordinates defined in VastDB, enabling assessment of both direct and indirect splicing regulation. CHARM additionally allows users to upload their own datasets to identify candidate RBPs relevant to their system. Together, CHARM and eCLIPSE provide a unified discovery platform for dissecting the regulatory landscape of RBPs in human disease contexts.
